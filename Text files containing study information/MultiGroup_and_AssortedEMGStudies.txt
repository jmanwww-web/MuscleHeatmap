Brief summaries (search and read abstract and onward for more info):
1. Analyzed core muscle activation across bent-over barbell row, seated cable row, and machine row, each bilaterally and unilaterally. Barbell row elicited the greatest erector spinae and multifidus activation. Unilateral variants of all motions significantly increased external oblique activation. 
2. In comparison of multiple grip types for pull-ups, pronated grip was superior for middle trapezius activation, while nearly all other relevant muscle groups were equally activated across variations. 
3. Compared incline lat pulldown, reverse pec deck, and seated rows for EMG of all three deltoid portions. No difference in anterior deltoid activation was observed across exercises. Reverse pec deck was by far the most effective for recruiting the posterior deltoid. Reinforces that reverse fly patterns cannot be adequately replaced by pulling exercises for training of posterior deltoids. 
4. Compared bench press, dumbbell fly, shoulder press, and lateral raise for all three deltoid portions. The lateral raise and shoulder press produced significantly more medial deltoid activation than other exercises. Posterior deltoids saw the most activation from lateral raises as well, with other exercises falling far behind. Affirms that pressing movements are effective only for the anterior portion of the deltoid, with over movements required for satisfactory activation of other portions. 
5. Compared activation of infraspinatus, teres minor, supraspinatus, posterior deltoid, middle deltoid during seven common shoulder external rotation exercises. Mean EMG activity for posterior deltoid was significantly greater for neutral arm position with resistance applied anteriorly at 90 degree abduction, supporting earlier findings about reduced fly motions inducing the most posterior deltoid activation.
